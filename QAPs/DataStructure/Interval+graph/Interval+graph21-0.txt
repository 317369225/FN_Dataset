Why does a derivative for the infinite power tower x^x^x^... exist?
Let's see what we can find out about this function.  Let                                     y=f(x)=x x x ⋯   .  Note that  y=x y ,    so that  x=f −1 (y)=y 1/y .    So we know the inverse function.  That should be enough to determine the derivative.  Here's a graph of  f −1   Note that it's not a one-to-one function.  It increases until y=e  then decreases.  The maximum value it takes on is  e 1/e ≈1.44668.  Probably the easiest way to find the derivative  dydx     is to implicitly differentiate the equation  y=x y .    First take logs to make the differentiation easier.                                               logy=ylogx  Then take  ddx                                         1y dydx =dydx logx+yx   Solve for  dydx     to get                        dydx =yx(1/y−logx) =x x x ⋯   x(1/x x x ⋯   −logx)   By the way, the original sequence of functions does converge               x,x x ,x x x  ,x x x x   ,…→f(x)=x x x ⋯     on the interval (e −e ,e 1/e ].  Here are the graphs of the first few functions On the interval  (e −e ,1)  the ones with the odd number of x's converge from below while the ones with an even number of x's converge from above.  On the interval (1,e 1/e ]  the terms all converge from below.
