Is it possible to solve the equation [math]x^3 - 6 x^2 + 3x + 10 = 0[/math] without the help of the hit-and-trial method?
Let us define [math]p (x) := x^3 - 6 x^2 + 3 x + 10[/math]. Do note that                               [math]p (-1) = -1 - 6 - 3 + 10 = 0[/math] Therefore, [math]-1[/math] is a root of [math]p[/math], i.e., we can factor [math]p[/math] as follows                               [math]p (x) = (x+1) (a x^2 + b x + c)[/math] We can easily find that [math]a = 1[/math], [math]b = -7[/math], and [math]c = 10[/math]. Therefore,                               [math]\begin{array}{rl} p (x) &= (x+1) (x^2 - 7 x + 10)\\\\ &= (x+1) (x-2) (x-5)\end{array}[/math] It is now easy to solve the equation [math]p (x) = 0[/math].
