Can somebody give me a clear and detailed proof of Sperner's Theorem?
Here is a step by step proof: Sperner Family: A family [math]F[/math] of sets is called a Sperner family if no member of [math]F[/math] is a subset of any other member of [math]F[/math], i.e. [math]A, B \in F \Rightarrow A \not\subset B[/math] and [math]B \not\subset A[/math]. Sperner's Theorem: Let [math]F[/math] be a Sperner family of subsets of an [math]n\text{-element}[/math] set [math]X[/math]. Then, [math]|F| \le {n \choose \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil} [/math] Proof: Claim 1: Consider a chain of subsets, a sequence [math]\phi = A_0 \subset A_1 \subset \dots \subset A_n = X[/math]. There are as many chains as permutations of [math]X[/math], that is, [math]n![/math] Proof: Let [math]\pi[/math] be any permutation, then we get a chain by setting [math]A_i = \{1\pi, 2\pi, \dots, i\pi \}[/math] for [math]i \in \{0, 1, \dots, n\}[/math]. Similarly, given a chain, we can uniquely recover the permutation since elements are added one at a time. Thus, there are as many chains as permutations i.e. [math]n![/math] Claim 2: The number of chains containing a given set [math]A[/math] where [math]|A| = k[/math] is [math]k! (n-k)![/math] Proof: If [math]|A| = k[/math], then it must happen that [math]A = A_k[/math]. So the chain containing [math]A[/math] is obtained by joining together a chain for [math]A[/math] and a chain for [math]X \setminus A[/math]. Thus, [math]A[/math] lies in [math]k! (n-k)![/math] permutations. Claim 3: Let [math]F[/math] be a Sperner family, then any chain contains at most one member of [math]F[/math] Proof: Assume that a chain contains more than one member of [math]F[/math], say [math]A,B[/math]. Since [math]A,B[/math] lie in a Sperner family, we know [math]A \not\subset B[/math] and [math]B \not\subset A[/math]. But this contradicts that [math]A,B[/math] lie in a chain, since any two sets [math]A,B[/math] in a chain satisfy [math]A \subset B[/math] or [math]B \subset A[/math]. Now back to the proof of Sperner's theorem. We know that any chain contains at most one set in [math]F[/math]. So the number of chains which contain a member of [math]F[/math] is [math]\sum_{A \in F} |A| ! (n - |A|) ! = n! (\sum_{A \in F} \frac{1}{{n \choose |A|}})[/math] Since, there are at most [math]n![/math] chains in total, we have [math]\sum_{A \in F} \frac{1}{{n \choose |A|}} \le 1[/math] Now, the middle binomial coefficients are the largest, so their reciprocals are the smallest. Thus, for any [math]A \in F[/math], [math]\frac{1}{{n \choose \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil}} \le \frac{1}{{n \choose |A|}}[/math]. So, [math]\sum_{A \in F} \frac{1}{{n \choose  \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil }} \le \sum_{A \in F} \frac{1}{{n \choose |A|}} \le 1[/math] [math]\sum_{A \in F} \frac{1}{{n \choose \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil}} \le 1[/math] [math]\frac{|F|}{{n \choose \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil}} \le 1[/math] [math]|F| \le {n \choose \lceil \frac{n}{2} \rceil} [/math] This proves the claim of Sperner's theorem.
