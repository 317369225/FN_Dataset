Are complex numbers and vectors over [math]\mathbb{R}^{2}[/math] (2-tuple) the same thing?
Logicians are very picky about things like this and they have a way that may explain the differences to your satisfaction.  They define various mathematical things not just as sets, but as sets along with their operations. The field [math]\mathbb C[/math] The complex numbers  [math]\mathbb C[/math] form a field, and a field can be defined as a set along with two binary operations that satisfy a certain list of axioms.  That means a field is not just a set  [math]S[/math]  but also has two binary operations  [math]+[/math]  and  [math]\cdot.[/math]  So it's really a triple [math](S,+,\cdot).[/math]  Thus, you can take the field of complex numbers to be the triple  [math](\mathbb R\times\mathbb R,+,\cdot)[/math]  where  [math]\mathbb R\times\mathbb R[/math]  stands for the set of ordered pairs of real numbers, [math]+[/math]  is coordinatewise addition, and  [math]\cdot[/math]  is the special multiplication defined for complex numbers:                               [math](x,y)\cdot(u,v)=(xu-yv,xv+yu)[/math] The vector space  [math]\mathbb R^2[/math] The vector space  [math]\mathbb R^2[/math]  is a vector space over the real field  [math]\mathbb R.[/math]   A vector space  [math]V[/math]  over  [math]\mathbb R[/math]  is also more than just a set  [math]V.[/math]  It's a set equipped with a binary operation  [math]+[/math]  and another operation  [math]\mathbb R\times V\to V[/math]  called scalar multiplication, which together satisfy another certain list of axioms.  So a real vector space is also a triple  [math](V,+,\cdot)[/math]  but this time  [math]\cdot[/math]  is scalar multiplication.  Thus, you can take the vector space [math]\mathbb R^2[/math]  to be the triple [math](\mathbb R\times\mathbb R,+,\cdot)[/math]  where  [math]\mathbb R\times\mathbb R[/math]  stands for the same set of ordered pairs of real numbers, [math]+[/math]  is the same coordinatewise addition, but this time  [math]\cdot[/math]  is scalar multiplication which takes one real number  [math]a[/math]  and a pair  [math](x,y)[/math]  as arguments                                     [math]a(x,y)=(ax,ay)[/math] [math]\mathbb C[/math] is a field extension of [math]\mathbb R[/math] [math]\mathbb C[/math]  has both these structures, and they're compatible.  That happens whenever you have one field which is an extension of another field, like  [math]\mathbb C[/math]  is a field extension of  [math]\mathbb R.[/math]
