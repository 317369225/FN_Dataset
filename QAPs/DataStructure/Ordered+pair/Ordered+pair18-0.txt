Let [math]a[/math] and [math]b[/math] both be positive integers less than or equal to 2015. For how many distinct ordered pairs [math](a,b)[/math] is the following expression also an integer? [math]\frac{a^2+b^2}{13}[/math]
This question is equivalent to asking how many pairs [math](a,b)[/math] fulfill [math]a^2+b^2 \mod 13=0[/math]. A striaghtforward computation (or equivalently some knowledge of algebraic number theory) shows, that exactly 6 of the numbers from 1 to 12 appear as one computes [math]x^2\mod 13[/math] for [math]x[/math] between 1 and 12. Furthermore observe, that if [math]a=0\mod 13[/math], then the only solution is [math]b=0\mod 13[/math]. Now for the computation: 2015=13*155. So there are 155 ways of choosing [math]a=0\mod 13[/math] and 2015-155=1860 ways of choosing [math]a\neq 0 \mod 13[/math].
