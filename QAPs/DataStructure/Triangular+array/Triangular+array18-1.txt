What is the clearest proof of the Spectral Theorem?
(This one from G. Strang's Linear Algebra and Its Application seems very clean and simple). Hermitian symmetry and unitary transform are two key features. Spectral Theorem: Every Hermitian matrix [math]A[/math]can be diagonalized by unitary matrix: [math] U^HAU = \Lambda [/math]. The columns of [math]U[/math] contain a complete set of orthonormal eigenvectors. You need Schur's lemma to prove it. Schur's lemma: For any square matrx [math]A[/math], there is a unitary matrix [math]U[/math] such that [math]U^HAU = T [/math] is a upper triangular. The eigenvalues of [math]A[/math] appears along the [math]T[/math]'s main diagonal. Firstly use Schur's lemma to prove Spectral theorem: 1. If [math]A[/math] is Hermitian so is [math]U^HAU[/math], [math](U^HAU)^H = U^HA^HU =U^HAU[/math]. 2. If Hermitain is also triangular, it must be diagonal. We have proved Spectral theorem. Now prove Schur's lemma: Let [math]A[/math] be a [math]n\times n[/math] matrix. We have at least one eigenvalue from [math]A[/math], let [math]Ax_1=\lambda_1x_1 [/math], where [math]\lambda_1[/math] is the eignevalue and [math]x_1[/math] is normal eigenvector. Put [math]x_1[/math] in the first column of [math]U_1[/math]. We complete the other columns of [math]U_1[/math] as normal bases (which can always be done by Gram-Schmidt process). We can write [math]AU_1=U_1\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} \lambda_1&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\end{array}\right] [/math]or, [math]U_1^HAU_1=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} \lambda_1&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\0&*&*&\cdots&*\end{array}\right][/math] At the second step works on the [math](n-1)\times (n-1)[/math] matrix in the lower rigth corner of [math]U_1^HAU_1[/math]. This matrix at least has one eigenvalue [math]\lambda_2[/math] and a unit eigenvector [math]x_2[/math]. We can construct [math]U_2[/math] [math]U_2=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} 1&0&0&\cdots&0\\0&\lambda_2&*&\cdots&*\\0&0&*&\cdots&*\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\0&0&*&\cdots&*\end{array}\right][/math]and, [math]U_2^H(U_1^HAU_1)U_2=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} \lambda_1&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&\lambda_2&*&\cdots&*\\0&0&*&\cdots&*\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\0&0&*&\cdots&*\end{array}\right][/math] You probably need to convince yourself that the right hand side is indeed the form after [math]U_2[/math] transform. Similarly we can show [math]U_n^H(\cdots(U_3^H(U_2^H(U_1^HAU_1)U_2)U_3)\cdots)U_n=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} \lambda_1&*&*&\cdots&*\\0&\lambda_2&*&\cdots&*\\0&0&\lambda_3&\cdots&*\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\0&0&0&\cdots&\lambda_n\end{array}\right][/math] or, [math]U^HAU = T[/math], where [math] U = U_1U_2U_3\cdots U_n[/math].
