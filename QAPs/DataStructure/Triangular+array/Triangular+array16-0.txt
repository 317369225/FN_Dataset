You started with the upper triangular matrix, [math]\mathbf{A} =\begin{bmatrix} i & c & 1\\ 0 & i & 2i \\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}[/math] The triangular matrix has the eigenvalues on the main diagonal which clearly does get you [math]i[/math],[math]i[/math] and [math]1[/math] and which cannot depend on [math]c[/math]. You let [math]c= 0[/math] saying it has to be that, I get instead [math]c=1[/math]. To show why I get [math]c=1[/math], take the eigenvalue of [math]\lambda=1[/math]. Writing out the simultaneous equations explicitly gives you, 1) --- [math]ix+cy+z=x[/math] 2) --- [math]0+iy+2iz=y[/math] 3) --- [math]0+0+z=z[/math] Solve 2) for z, recalling that [math]-i=\frac{1}{i}[/math] gets you 2a) [math]z=\frac{-y-iy}{2}[/math] Substitute 2a) into 1) to get, 1a) --- [math]ix+cy+\frac{-y-iy}{2}=x[/math] Which when factored, becomes, [math]ix+(c-\frac{1}{2}) y -\frac{iy}{2}=x[/math] Which means, subtracting [math]ix[/math] from both sides of the above, you get,Â  [math](x-ix) = (c-\frac{1}{2}) y -i\frac{y}{2}[/math] and such that the real and imaginary parts must be equal. Setting the real parts equal gets you [math](c-\frac{1}{2})y=x[/math]. Setting imaginary parts equal gets you [math]-i \frac{y}{2}=-ix[/math] which means [math]\frac{1}{2}y = x[/math]. For both the real and imaginary statements above to be true at the same time, [math](c-\frac{1}{2})y=\frac{1}{2}y[/math] must hold true, so, [math]c=1[/math] follows.
