Let f(x)=ax 2 +bx+c  be a quadratic polynomial where a,b,c  are real numbers. How can it be proved that f(f(x))=x  has no real solution?
Let us consider the existentially quantified formula                                                   ∃x((f∘f)(x)=x)  Since f(x):=ax 2 +bx+c  , we obtain the following predicate                     Φ(a,b,c):=∃x(a(f(x)) 2 +bf(x)+c=x)  where a,b,c  are free variables and where x  is a bound variable. Let us assume that these four variables live in R  . Performing quantifier elimination [0], we obtain the following equivalent quantifier-free formula in disjunctive normal form (DNF)                     Φ(a,b,c) ≡(a=0∧b=1∧c=0)∨(a=0∧b≠1)∨(a=0∧ac+b+1=0)∨(a≠0∧4ac−b 2 +2b−1≤0)∨(a≠0∧4ac−b 2 +2b+3≤0)   The cases where a=0  are utterly uninteresting, for we want the polynomial function f  to be quadratic. Hence, in the cases where a≠0  , we have                     Φ(a≠0,b,c) ≡(4ac−b 2 +2b−1≤0)∨(4ac−b 2 +2b+3≤0)   Note that the discriminant of the quadratic polynomial ax 2 +bx+c  is                                                   Δ(a,b,c):=b 2 −4ac  Hence, we obtain                          Φ(a≠0,b,c) ≡(Δ(a,b,c)≥2b−1)∨(Δ(a,b,c)≥2b+3)   or, equivalently,                                    Δ(a,b,c)≥min{2b−1,2b+3}  To make the function f∘f  lack a fixed point, choose a triple (a,b,c)  , with a≠0  , such that                                    Δ(a,b,c)<min{2b−1,2b+3}  For example, let us pick b=1  . Then, min{2b−1,2b+3}=1  . We obtain the inequality ac>0  . If we choose a=c=1  , we obtain the polynomial function f(x)=x 2 +x+1  , and f∘f  lacks a fixed point.
